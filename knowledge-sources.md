# Knowledge Sources

This file lists the sources used by the Open Research Workflow Agent to surface relevant guidance and signpost researchers to appropriate resources. The agent will only reference sources included here — it does not draw on outside knowledge. The list is intentionally curated rather than exhaustive, prioritising reputable, widely-used resources relevant to UK research practice. It is designed to be configurable: deployers are encouraged to add or substitute sources appropriate to their institutional context, provided those sources are publicly available.

A comprehensive guide to stakeholder engagement as part of project and programme delivery, covering identification and analysis of stakeholders, planning engagement activities, managing resistance, and building trusted relationships. Useful for researchers planning projects that involve external partners, communities, or societal actors, particularly where there are multiple groups with different interests or levels of influence. Applicable across disciplines; not health-specific.
- [Government Design Principles](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/government-design-principles) (Government Digital Service, GOV.UK)
Eleven principles for designing public-facing services and digital tools, centred on user needs, openness, accessibility and iteration. Particularly relevant for researchers producing digital outputs — tools, dashboards, data portals, or web resources — intended for use by others. Principle 10 ("Make things open: it makes things better") directly reinforces open research values around sharing code, data, and designs. Suitable for researchers at any technical confidence level.

## Scholarly articles and literature

- [The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management and stewardship](https://www.nature.com/articles/sdata201618) (Wilkinson et al., Scientific Data, 2016)
The foundational paper defining the FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable). Relevant to any researcher whose project generates data or other digital outputs. Useful for planning stage discussions around data management and sharing. Suitable for all researcher types.
- [The past, present and future of Registered Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01629-y) (Chambers & Tzavella, Nature Human Behaviour, 2021)
Reviews the Registered Reports publication format, in which study plans are peer reviewed before research begins. Relevant to researchers planning empirical studies who want to reduce publication bias and improve transparency. Abstract only accessible — verify full text availability before including.
- [Reproducibility: How Can we Make Sure That Research Studies Can be Trusted?](https://kids.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/frym.2026.1582084/full) (Dudda et al., Frontiers for Young Minds, 2026)
An accessible, plain-language introduction to reproducibility and open science practices. Useful for researchers new to open research concepts or those wanting a non-technical starting point. Suitable for all disciplines and confidence levels.
- https://press.lse.ac.uk/books/e/10.31389/lsepress.dos
- [Computational Reproducibility: A Primer from UKRN](https://zenodo.org/records/192328910) (Schultze et al., Zenodo, 2026)
A practical, accessible introduction to computational reproducibility — the ability to obtain the same results from the same data and procedures. Covers common pitfalls such as ambiguous method descriptions, software version changes, and missing packages, with guidance on mitigating them. Also introduces the FAIR principles in the context of computational workflows. Relevant to any researcher using computational methods in data processing or analysis. No prior technical expertise required; suitable for all confidence levels.
- [A Tale of Open Science: Emergence of a New Normal](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s41471-025-00218-5) (Soliman et al., Schmalenbach Journal of Business Research, 2025)
An open access article tracing the emergence of open science as a response to the replication crisis, originally in psychology and now spreading to business research and other fields. Covers key practices including preregistration, registered reports, open data, and open peer review, with honest discussion of their limitations. Useful background reading for researchers new to the open science landscape, particularly those in social sciences or business-adjacent fields. Suitable for planning stage conversations about why open research practices matter.
- [Seven Easy Steps to Open Science: An Annotated Reading List](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1027/2151-2604/a000387#_i4) (Crüwell et al., Zeitschrift für Psychologie, 2019)
An annotated guide to seven core open science topics: open access, open data and materials, reproducible analyses, preregistration, registered reports, replication research, and teaching open science. Each section summarises a key paper and suggests further reading. Discipline-agnostic in approach and written for researchers and students with no prior background in open science. A useful signposting resource for researchers at the planning stage who want to understand the landscape before deciding where to focus.
